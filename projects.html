On the book L'Homme glial, Y. Agid and P. Magistretti : their text presents advances during the last decade in understanding the role of glial cells in the brain, and in particular astrocytes, revealing that these cells are not only auxiliary cells for neurons, providing energy and evacuating lethal excesses of neurotransmitters, but are also fundamental for information processing in the brain. Amongst the functions in which astrocytes are involved are for instance the control of neurogenesis, the acceleration or inhibition of neurotransmission, and synchronization amongst groups of neurons. Moreover, they are opposed to neurons in their information transmission mode, thus providing elements for the reflexion on trade-off between wire and diffusive modes in the brain. Since they are involved in computing, one could wonder about the computational power of these cells; this could be put in relation with recent evidence that a single cell, called Amoebe, can solve some NP-hard problem in linear time [ref], while neurons are thought to execute computation in networks. For symbolic dynamicians, neurons and glia could be thought as forming two superimposed layers of autonomous and interacting computations.




On the book L'intelligence humaine n'est pas un algorithme, O. Houdé: the author, motivated by education, draws perspectives for a re-definition of intelligence, articulating the work of central figures of cognitive sciences, J. Piaget, D. Kahneman and A. Damasio, and the mechanism on inhibition. J. Piaget was renowned for his theory on the development of intelligence in the childhood, based on a distinction of stages separated by the acquisition of certain computational mechanisms. In this process, each stage completely overcomes the previous one, as if logics progressively replaces intuitions for a better understanding and adaptation to the environment. D. Kahneman provided another view on intelligence, by stating - an idea that structured all his production - the division of the human brain into two sub-systems: one has fast execution but is sometimes unprecise (it consists of beliefs), and the second one is slow but exact (and consists more of logics). In particular, this contradicts J. Piaget's theory of intelligence stages since the logical and the unlogical coexist in the adult brain, which was shown in experiments by D. Kahneman and collaborators. O. Houdé states himself as in the following of this stream of ideas, and that intelligence is not a matter of how complex thought can be in the brain, but of organisation in the brain. His vision includes a third system which plays the role of an arbitrator between the two other sub-systems, inhibiting the first one to allow the execution of the second one. This third sub-system is related to emotions, thinking them, as A. Damasio, as a guide for reasoning, articulating the two first sub-systems. The author analyses the importance of inhibition in the brain, in particular in visual exploration (return inhibition, notion of M. Posner), and argues that inhibition does not defines consciousness, since there are some unconscious inhibitions (S. Van Gaal's experiments).
